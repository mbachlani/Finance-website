Maximizing Your RESP: A Complete Guide for Canadian Parents

Malik Bachlani
June 15, 2023
8 min read

As a Canadian parent, one of the most powerful tools available to help fund your child's education is the Registered Education Savings Plan (RESP). This comprehensive guide will walk you through everything you need to know to maximize your RESP benefits and give your child the best possible start in their academic journey.

What is an RESP?

A Registered Education Savings Plan (RESP) is a tax-advantaged savings account designed specifically for education funding. The primary benefits of an RESP include:

  • Government grants: The Canada Education Savings Grant (CESG) matches 20% of your contributions, up to $500 per year and $7,200 lifetime per child
  • Tax-deferred growth: Investments grow tax-free until withdrawal
  • Tax-efficient withdrawals: When used for education, the growth portion is taxed in the student's hands (typically resulting in little to no tax)

Key Takeaway

Starting an RESP early and contributing consistently allows you to maximize government grants and benefit from decades of tax-sheltered compound growth.

Maximizing Government Grants

The Canada Education Savings Grant (CESG) is the primary incentive for RESP contributions. Here's how to get the most from it:

Basic CESG

The government provides a 20% match on the first $2,500 contributed annually, up to $500 per year. To maximize this:

  • Contribute at least $2,500 per child each year to get the full $500 grant
  • If you can't contribute $2,500 annually, even smaller amounts will qualify for partial grants
  • Unused grant room carries forward, but you can only catch up on one missed year at a time

Additional CESG for Lower-Income Families

Families with modest incomes may qualify for additional CESG:

  • First tier: 10% extra on first $500 contributed (total 30% match) for family income under $50,197
  • Second tier: 20% extra on first $500 contributed (total 40% match) for family income under $100,392

Canada Learning Bond

For lower-income families, the Canada Learning Bond provides:

  • $500 initial payment
  • $100 annually for each eligible year, up to age 15
  • Maximum $2,000 per child
  • No contributions required to receive this money

Investment Strategies for Your RESP

How you invest your RESP contributions can significantly impact the final amount available for education. Consider these approaches:

Age-Based Investing

This strategy automatically adjusts risk as the child approaches post-secondary age:

  • Early years (0-10): More aggressive (80-100% equities)
  • Middle years (11-14): Balanced approach (50-70% equities)
  • Final years (15+): Conservative (20-40% equities)

Portfolio Allocation Examples

Child's Age Aggressive Moderate Conservative

Withdrawal Strategies

When it's time to use the RESP funds, understanding withdrawal rules can help minimize taxes:

Educational Assistance Payments (EAPs)

These consist of government grants and investment growth:

  • Taxed in the student's hands (usually resulting in little or no tax)
  • Maximum $8,000 in first 13 weeks of full-time studies ($4,000 for part-time)
  • Unlimited amounts after initial period

Post-Secondary Education (PSE) Withdrawals

These are returns of your original contributions:

  • Not taxable (since contributions were made with after-tax dollars)
  • No limits on amounts that can be withdrawn

Pro Tip

Withdraw EAPs (grants and growth) first to maximize tax benefits, as these amounts must be used for education or returned to the government.

What If Your Child Doesn't Pursue Higher Education?

There are several options if your child chooses not to attend post-secondary school:

  • Transfer to another child: You can transfer the RESP to another eligible child
  • Withdraw contributions: You can withdraw your original contributions tax-free
  • Transfer to RRSP: Up to $50,000 of growth can be transferred to your RRSP if you have contribution room
  • Close the RESP: Government grants must be returned, and growth is taxed as income plus 20% penalty

Common RESP Mistakes to Avoid

Many parents inadvertently reduce their RESP benefits through these common errors:

  • Starting too late: Missing out on years of compound growth and grants
  • Under-contributing: Not maximizing the 20% government match
  • Over-contributing: The $50,000 lifetime limit doesn't include grants
  • Poor investment choices: Too conservative early on or too aggressive later
  • Not naming a successor subscriber: Important in case of death of primary subscriber

Next Steps

Setting up and managing an RESP is one of the best financial gifts you can give your child. To get started or optimize your existing RESP:

  1. Determine your monthly contribution amount to maximize grants
  2. Select an appropriate investment strategy based on your child's age
  3. Set up automatic contributions to ensure consistency
  4. Review your RESP annually to adjust investments and confirm you're on track
